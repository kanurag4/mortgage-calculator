About this tool

Enter your loan amount, interest rate, and term to see your repayment, total interest, and the full amortisation schedule. Switch between Principal & Interest and Interest Only to compare strategies.

Add an offset account balance or extra repayments to see exactly how much interest you save and how many years you shave off the loan. The frequency comparison table shows the benefit of paying weekly or fortnightly versus monthly.

Frequently asked questions

How is my repayment calculated?

Your repayment uses the standard amortisation formula: P × r × (1+r)ⁿ / ((1+r)ⁿ − 1), where P is the loan balance, r is the per-period interest rate, and n is the total number of periods. For a 30-year loan at 6% paid monthly, that's r = 0.06/12 and n = 360.

What is an offset account and how does it work?

An offset account is a transaction account linked to your mortgage. The balance in it reduces the principal on which interest is calculated each period — so a $500,000 loan with a $50,000 offset accrues interest on only $450,000. The loan balance itself is unchanged; you're just reducing the effective amount on which interest compounds.

Does paying fortnightly or weekly actually save money?

Yes — but the saving comes from making the equivalent of 13 monthly payments a year rather than 12. A fortnightly repayment is half the monthly amount, paid 26 times, which totals one extra monthly payment annually. That extra principal reduces the balance sooner, cutting total interest. The frequency comparison table shows exactly how much you save and how many years earlier you pay off.

How do extra repayments reduce my loan?

Any amount above the minimum repayment goes directly to reducing the principal. Because future interest is calculated on a lower balance, you pay less interest every subsequent period — and the effect compounds over time. Even a small extra amount each month can save tens of thousands in interest over a 30-year loan.

When does interest-only make sense?

Interest-only loans are common for investment properties where the investor wants to maximise cash flow or tax deductions in the short term. During the IO period you pay no principal, so the balance doesn't decrease — once the IO period ends, your P&I repayments are recalculated on the full original balance over the remaining term, meaning they'll be higher than if you'd been repaying principal all along.
